Assumptions about the signal

There is a general assumption, based on observations, that vibrations of a healthy machine can be modeled as Gaussian white noise. In the presence of a fault, periodically repeating impulsive components appear within the noise.

Example of a healthy signal and a severely damaged signal.

Spectrogram

A spectrogram is defined as the magnitude of the Short-Time Fourier Transform: \( |STFT(t,f)| \). The STFT illustrates how the frequency spectrum of a signal evolves over time. The values in the spectrogram correspond to the amplitudes of individual frequency components within specific time intervals.

In the computations, a discrete version of the equation is used. For observations \( X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N \), a time instant \( t \in T \), and a frequency \( f \in F \): \[ STFT(t,f) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X_k w(t-k)e^{2j\pi fk/N}. \]

It is worth noting that for a signal corresponding to white noise, the spectrogram values in each time interval and for every frequency will also exhibit characteristics of white noise. This implies that no distinct patterns or prominent frequency bands should be observable. Otherwise, the presence of such anomalies may indicate a fault.

Selectors

A selector is defined as a statistic that enables the identification of the informative frequency band (IFB) based on the spectrogram representation of the signal. After computing the spectrogram, the values of these statistics can be evaluated for each sub-signal. For frequency bands that do not contain impulses, the selector values remain relatively constant, whereas in bands containing impulses, noticeable changes in the selector values can be observed, corresponding to the presence of an impulsive signal.

  • Spectral Kurtosis

    • Spectral Kurtosis is a frequency-domain measure used to detect deviations from Gaussian behavior in a signal. For a purely Gaussian (noise-like) process, the spectral kurtosis remains close to zero across all frequencies. The presence of impulsive components causes significant positive deviations, allowing frequency bands containing transient events to be identified. It is defined by the formula:

    \[ SK(f) = \#T\frac{\sum _{t \in T} |STFT(t,f)|^4}{(\sum _{t \in T}|STFT(t,f)|^2)^2} -2, \]

  • Jarque-Bera statistic

    • The Jarque-Bera statistic is a classical goodness-of-fit test that jointly evaluates skewness and kurtosis to assess whether a signal follows a Gaussian distribution. In the context of spectrogram-based analysis, large values of the statistic indicate frequency bands where the distribution of spectral amplitudes departs from normality due to impulsive behavior. It is defined by the formula:

    \[ JB(f) = \frac{\#T}{6}(S(f)^2 + \frac{(K(f)-1)^2}{4}), \]

    where \( S(f) \) and \( K(f) \) denote the empirical skewness and kurtosis, respectively, computed for the sub-signal corresponding to the given frequency \( f \).

  • Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic

    • The Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic measures the maximum discrepancy between the empirical cumulative distribution function of the observed data and a reference theoretical distribution. Here, it is used to quantify how strongly the distribution of spectral coefficients at a given frequency differs from the assumed Gaussian model. It is defined by the formula:

    \[ KSS(f) = \text{sup} _x|ECDF(f,x)-\Phi(f,x)|, \]

    where

    \[ \Phi(f,x) = \int^x_{-\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \hat{\sigma(f)}}^2 } \text{exp} (-\frac{(x-\hat{\mu(f)})^{2^2}}{2\hat{\sigma(f)}}) dx, \]

    and

    \[ ECDF(f,x) = \frac{1}{\#T} \sum_{t \in T}\mathrm{1}\{|STFT(t_k, f)| \leq x \}. \]

  • Cramer von Mises statistic family

    • The Cramér-von Mises family of statistics evaluates the global discrepancy between empirical and theoretical distributions by integrating the squared difference over the entire range of values. By introducing a weighting function, this family allows greater sensitivity to deviations occurring in specific regions of the distribution, such as the tails. It is defined by the formula:

    \[ Q(f) = \# T \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (ECDF(f,x) - \Phi(f,x))^2\phi(x)dx, \]

    where \( \phi(x) \) is a weighting function applied to the distances. If \( \phi(x) = 1 \), the statistic is referred to as the Cramér-von Mises (CVM) statistic, whereas if \( \phi(x) = [\Phi(f,x)(1-\Phi(f,x))]^{-1} \), the statistic is known as the Anderson-Darling (AD) statistic.

  • Conditional Variance statistic

    • The Conditional Variance statistic is designed to detect changes in variability between different regions of the distribution. By comparing variances computed in the central part and in the tails of the distribution, this selector highlights frequency bands where impulsive components increase dispersion outside the expected Gaussian core. It is defined by the formula:

    \[ N:=\frac{1}{\rho}(\frac{\hat{\sigma}^2_{L_q}-\hat{\sigma}_{M_q}^2}{\hat{\sigma}^2}+\frac{\hat{\sigma}_{R_q}^2-\hat{\sigma}_{M_q}^2}{\hat{\sigma}^2})\sqrt{n}, \]

    where

    \[ L_q := ( -\infty, \Phi_{\mu,\sigma}^{-1}(q)] \] \[ R_q := [\Phi_{\mu,\sigma}^{-1}(1-q), \infty) \] \[ M_q := (\Phi_{\mu,\sigma}^{-1}(q),\Phi_{\mu,\sigma}^{-1}(1-q)), \]

    are the lower, upper and middle q-quantiles of the dataset.

Filtration and diagnosis

Based on the identified IFB, the signal was band-pass filtered by suppressing all frequency components outside the detected range. Impulse occurrences in the filtered signal \( X_f[n] \) were detected using a simple statistical threshold: \[ T = \mu_{X_f} + 3\sigma_{X_f}, \] where \( \mu_{X_f} \) and \( \sigma_{X_f} \) denote the mean and standard deviation of the filtered signal, respectively. This threshold allows for reliable detection of high-amplitude impulses while remaining insensitive to background noise. Finally, the impulse strength was quantified as the ratio of the mean energy of impulse samples to the mean energy of the entire filtered signal: \[ M = \frac{\mathbb{E}\left(X_{\text{imp}}^2\right)}{\mathbb{E}\left(X_f^2\right)}. \]
