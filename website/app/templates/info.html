Assumptions about the signal

There is a general assumption, based on observations, that vibrations of a healthy machine can be modeled as Gaussian white noise. In the presence of a fault, periodically repeating impulsive components appear within the noise.

Spectrogram

A spectrogram is defined as the magnitude of the Short-Time Fourier Transform: \( |STFT(t,f)| \). The STFT illustrates how the frequency spectrum of a signal evolves over time. The values in the spectrogram correspond to the amplitudes of individual frequency components within specific time intervals.

In the discrete setting, for observations \( X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N \), a time index \( t \in T \), and a frequency \( f \in F \), the STFT is defined as \[ STFT(t,f) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X_k\, w(t-k)\, e^{2\pi i f k / N}, \] where \( w(\cdot) \) denotes a windowing function.

For a signal corresponding to white noise, the spectrogram values at each time instant and frequency also resemble white noise, resulting in the absence of persistent structures or dominant frequency bands. The appearance of localized patterns or bands therefore indicates deviations from the noise model and may be interpreted as a signature of damage.

Selectors

A selector is defined as a statistic that enables the identification of the informative frequency band (IFB) based on the spectrogram representation of the signal. After computing the spectrogram, the values of these statistics can be evaluated for each sub-signal. For frequency bands that do not contain impulses, the selector values remain relatively constant, whereas in bands containing impulses, noticeable changes in the selector values can be observed, corresponding to the presence of an impulsive signal.

  • Spectral Kurtosis

    • Spectral Kurtosis quantifies deviations from Gaussian behavior in the frequency domain. For a purely Gaussian process, its value remains close to zero across all frequencies. The presence of impulsive components produces positive peaks, enabling the localization of frequency bands associated with transient events.

    \[ SK(f) = \#T \, \frac{\sum_{t \in T} |STFT(t,f)|^4}{\left(\sum_{t \in T} |STFT(t,f)|^2\right)^2} - 2. \]

  • Jarque–Bera statistic

    • The Jarque–Bera statistic is a classical goodness-of-fit measure that jointly evaluates skewness and kurtosis in order to assess normality. In spectrogram-based analysis, large values indicate frequency bands where the distribution of spectral amplitudes departs from the Gaussian assumption due to impulsive behavior.

    \[ JB(f) = \frac{\#T}{6} \left( S(f)^2 + \frac{(K(f)-1)^2}{4} \right), \]

    where \( S(f) \) and \( K(f) \) denote the empirical skewness and kurtosis computed for the sub-signal corresponding to frequency \( f \).

  • Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic

    • The Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic measures the maximum distance between the empirical cumulative distribution function (ECDF) of the data and a reference theoretical distribution. It provides a sensitive measure of local discrepancies between observed and modeled behavior.

    \[ KSS(f) = \sup_x \left| ECDF(f,x) - \Phi(f,x) \right|, \]

    where \( \Phi(f,x) \) denotes the cumulative distribution function of a Gaussian distribution with mean \( \hat{\mu}(f) \) and variance \( \hat{\sigma}^2(f) \), \[ \Phi(f,x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \hat{\sigma}^2(f)}} \exp\!\left( -\frac{(u-\hat{\mu}(f))^2}{2\hat{\sigma}^2(f)} \right) du, \] and \[ ECDF(f,x) = \frac{1}{\#T} \sum_{t \in T} \mathbf{1}\{ |STFT(t,f)| \le x \}. \]

  • Cramér–von Mises statistic family

    • The Cramér–von Mises family evaluates the global discrepancy between empirical and theoretical distributions by integrating the squared difference over the entire range of values. Weighting functions can be introduced to emphasize deviations in specific regions, such as the distribution tails.

    \[ Q(f) = \#T \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \left( ECDF(f,x) - \Phi(f,x) \right)^2 \phi(x) \, dx. \]

    If \( \phi(x) = 1 \), the statistic reduces to the Cramér–von Mises (CVM) statistic, whereas \( \phi(x) = [\Phi(f,x)(1-\Phi(f,x))]^{-1} \) yields the Anderson–Darling (AD) statistic.

  • Conditional Variance statistic

    • The Conditional Variance statistic detects changes in dispersion between the central and tail regions of the distribution. By comparing variances across these regions, it highlights frequency bands where impulsive components increase variability beyond the Gaussian core.

    \[ N = \frac{1}{\rho} \left( \frac{\hat{\sigma}^2_{L_q} - \hat{\sigma}^2_{M_q}}{\hat{\sigma}^2} + \frac{\hat{\sigma}^2_{R_q} - \hat{\sigma}^2_{M_q}}{\hat{\sigma}^2} \right) \sqrt{n}, \]

    where \( L_q \), \( M_q \), and \( R_q \) denote the lower, middle, and upper quantile-based regions of the distribution, respectively.

Filtration and diagnosis

Based on the identified IFB, the signal was band-pass filtered by suppressing all frequency components outside the detected range.

Filtered healthy signal

Filtered severely damaged signal

Impulse occurrences in the filtered signal \( X_f[n] \) were detected using a simple statistical threshold: \[ T = \mu_{X_f} + 3\sigma_{X_f}, \] where \( \mu_{X_f} \) and \( \sigma_{X_f} \) denote the mean and standard deviation of the filtered signal, respectively. This threshold allows for reliable detection of high-amplitude impulses while remaining insensitive to background noise. Finally, the impulse strength was quantified as the ratio of the mean energy of impulse samples to the mean energy of the entire filtered signal: \[ M = \frac{\mathbb{E}\left(X_{\text{imp}}^2\right)}{\mathbb{E}\left(X_f^2\right)}. \]

Based on observations, a threshold value of \(M_t=0.025\) was adopted to enable automatic detection of the presence of impulses in the signal.
